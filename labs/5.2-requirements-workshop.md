# Lab 5.2 – Requirements Workshop: User Stories & Acceptance Criteria

**Duration:** ~40 minutes  
**Format:** Individual or pair exercise  

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab you will be able to:

- Extract requirements from stakeholder interview transcripts
- Distinguish functional requirements from non-functional requirements
- Write user stories in standard format (*As a [role], I want [action], so that [outcome]*)
- Define clear acceptance criteria using Given/When/Then structure
- Recognize when a requirement is too vague to act on

---

## Background

Discovery tells you what exists. Requirements tell you what needs to change — and what success looks like when it does.

In practice, requirements rarely arrive fully formed. They emerge from conversations: stakeholders describe pain, describe goals, describe constraints. The BA's job is to listen carefully, ask follow-up questions, and translate what they hear into requirements that are specific enough for a development team to act on.

In this lab you will review transcripts from three stakeholder interviews conducted after the ORDPROC discovery exercise. Your job is to extract requirements, categorize them, and write user stories with acceptance criteria.

---

## The Scenario

Northstar Retail has decided to invest in improving the ORDPROC system based on the discovery findings. You have been asked to lead a requirements workshop. Before the group session, you conducted individual interviews with three stakeholders. Their transcripts are below.

---

## Stakeholder Interviews

### Interview 1 — Marcus Chen, Operations Manager

> **BA:** What is your biggest frustration with the current system?
>
> **Marcus:** Honestly? The surprises. I come in at 7:00 AM and I have no idea whether the batch job ran clean last night or not until someone manually checks the log. Half the time I find out there was a problem because a warehouse supervisor called me — not because any system told me. That's backwards.
>
> **BA:** What would better look like for you?
>
> **Marcus:** I want a notification the moment the job finishes — success or failure. And if it fails, I want to know why, not just that it failed. If it's going to run long, I want a heads-up before it misses the 06:00 cutoff, not after.
>
> **BA:** How long do you have to respond to a failure before it causes a real problem?
>
> **Marcus:** If I know by 04:00 that there's an issue, I can usually reach the warehouse supervisor in time to hold the 06:00 run. After that, it's damage control. So my threshold is: alert me by 04:00 if anything looks wrong.
>
> **BA:** What about the error log — orders that fail validation? How often do you look at it?
>
> **Marcus:** Every morning, but it takes about 45 minutes to work through it manually. We're looking at maybe 200–400 failed orders on a busy day. I'd love a way to bulk-retry the ones that failed for recoverable reasons — like an inventory sync issue — without re-running the whole batch.

---

### Interview 2 — Sarah Kim, Software Engineer (ORDPROC Maintainer)

> **BA:** What do you think is the most important thing to fix from a technical standpoint?
>
> **Sarah:** The lack of structured logging. Right now the job writes to a flat text file. There is no error code, no structured format, no correlation ID. When something goes wrong I'm reading raw text to figure out what happened. It takes forever.
>
> **BA:** How would structured logging help?
>
> **Sarah:** It would make filtering and automation possible. Right now nothing can read that log programmatically. If we had structured output — even just JSON or CSV — we could build dashboards, automated retries, alerting. Right now we have none of that.
>
> **BA:** Is there anything about the current system that you think works well and shouldn't be touched?
>
> **Sarah:** The validation logic itself is pretty solid. It's been tuned over years. I'd want to keep that — just modernize the plumbing around it.
>
> **BA:** What would you need to feel confident deploying a new version of this job?
>
> **Sarah:** A test environment that mirrors production data volumes. Right now we test against maybe 10% of real load. When something breaks at scale we find out at 2:00 AM. I need to be able to run a full-volume test before any release.

---

### Interview 3 — Elena Torres, Customer Service Lead

> **BA:** What do customers complain about most related to orders?
>
> **Elena:** Shipping delays, but specifically the mystery of it. They placed an order, they got a confirmation email, and then — nothing. No status update until the package actually ships, which could be two days later. They call us asking what happened.
>
> **BA:** What would reduce the volume of those calls?
>
> **Elena:** An automated status notification after the order is processed — not just the initial confirmation. Something like: "Your order has been confirmed and sent to our warehouse." If they know their order is in the system, they stop calling.
>
> **BA:** How quickly after placing an order do customers expect to hear something?
>
> **Elena:** Based on what I hear from customers, same day is the expectation. If someone places an order at 8:00 PM and hasn't heard anything by the next morning, that's when they start calling. So I'd say: they expect a processing confirmation before they wake up.
>
> **BA:** Have you had situations where a customer was told their order was confirmed, but it never actually got processed?
>
> **Elena:** Yes. That's the worst case — they get the initial order confirmation from the website, but their order lands in the error log and never makes it to the warehouse. No one tells them. They find out when they call three days later. That needs to stop.

### Step 2 — Categorize Requirements (5 minutes)

Review your list and sort each requirement into one of two categories:

- **Functional** — describes what the system should *do* (a feature, a behavior, an action)
- **Non-Functional** — describes a quality the system should *have* (performance, reliability, observability, testability)

Write the requirement number next to the appropriate category below.

> **Not sure which category?** Ask yourself: if you built the feature but it performed poorly, would the requirement still be met? If yes, it's functional. If no — because speed or reliability was part of the requirement — it's non-functional.

### Step 3 — Write User Stories (15 minutes)

Choose **four** requirements from your list — at least one functional and at least one non-functional — and write a user story for each one.

Use the format:

### Step 4 — Add Acceptance Criteria (10 minutes)

For each of your four stories, write **at least two acceptance criteria** using Given/When/Then format.

Acceptance criteria answer the question: *How will we know this story is done and working correctly?*

```
Given [a starting condition]
When  [an action or event occurs]
Then  [the expected observable outcome]
```

> **Watch out for vague criteria.** "The system should be fast" is not testable. "The batch job completes within 3 hours" is. Every acceptance criterion should have a clear pass/fail outcome.

## Summary

In this lab you:

- Extracted requirements from realistic stakeholder interview transcripts representing different roles and perspectives
- Distinguished functional requirements (what the system does) from non-functional requirements (how well it does it)
- Wrote user stories that connect features to business outcomes
- Defined testable acceptance criteria in Given/When/Then format

The user stories and acceptance criteria you wrote in this lab become the inputs to the next step: planning and tracking the work — and eventually validating that the deployed solution actually meets the requirements you defined.

## Discussion Questions

If time allows, discuss these with your group or reflect on them individually:

1. Marcus's requirement — "alert me by 04:00 if anything looks wrong" — contains a specific time. Is this a good acceptance criterion, or does it create unrealistic constraints? How would you refine it?
2. Elena described a case where customers receive an order confirmation but the order never gets processed. Is this a bug to fix or a new requirement to write? What's the difference, and does it matter?
3. Sarah wants a full-volume test environment. This is a non-functional requirement on the *development process*, not the production system. Should it go in the same backlog as the other stories? How would you handle it?
