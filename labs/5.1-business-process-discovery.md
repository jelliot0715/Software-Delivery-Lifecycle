# Lab 5.1 – Business Process Discovery

**Duration:** ~35 minutes  
**Format:** Individual or pair exercise

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab you will be able to:

- Identify stakeholders, inputs, outputs, and dependencies in a business process
- Recognize how mainframe batch jobs fit into a broader operational workflow
- Document a current-state process in plain business language
- Surface risks and pain points before any modernization work begins

---

## Background

Before a Business Analyst can gather requirements or propose improvements, they must understand what is actually happening today. This is called **current-state discovery** — and it is often harder than it sounds.

Processes that have existed for years are rarely well-documented. The people who know how they work are scattered across teams. Everyone has a partial view. The BA's job is to assemble the full picture by asking the right questions, reviewing available documentation, and synthesizing what they learn into something the entire team can work from.

In this lab you will play the role of a BA who has just been assigned to a legacy order processing system. You have been given a process description from a recent systems review. Your job is to complete a structured discovery worksheet and write a brief current-state summary.

---

## The Scenario

**Company:** Northstar Retail  
**System:** ORDPROC — Nightly Order Processing Batch Job  
**Platform:** IBM z/OS mainframe

---

### Process Description

Read the following description of Northstar Retail's order processing system. It was written by a systems administrator during an annual IT audit and represents the most up-to-date documentation available.

---

> *ORDPROC runs nightly at 01:00 UTC, Monday through Saturday. The job reads a flat file of customer orders accumulated throughout the day from the online storefront and the in-store point-of-sale (POS) system. It validates each order against the current inventory database, calculates shipping costs based on customer region and order weight, and writes approved orders to the fulfillment queue. Orders that fail validation are written to an error log file.*
>
> *The fulfillment queue is read by the warehouse management system (WMS) the following morning at 06:00 UTC, which generates pick lists for warehouse staff. Any orders not in the fulfillment queue by 06:00 are excluded from same-day processing and treated as next-day.*
>
> *ORDPROC typically completes within 2–3 hours. On high-volume days (post-holiday, major sales events), the job has been observed running 4–5 hours, occasionally missing the 06:00 WMS cutoff. When this occurs, operations staff manually notify the warehouse supervisor via email.*
>
> *The error log is reviewed manually each morning by the operations team. There is no automated alerting when orders fail validation. The estimated error rate is 3–5% of daily orders.*
>
> *The inventory database used by ORDPROC is a separate system from the real-time inventory display shown to customers on the storefront website. The two systems synchronize once daily at 23:30 UTC, 90 minutes before ORDPROC runs. If a customer purchases an item between 23:30 and 01:00, the inventory shown to ORDPROC may not reflect that purchase.*

## Challenge

You discovered that the inventory database used by ORDPROC synchronizes with the storefront at 23:30 UTC — 90 minutes before ORDPROC runs. This means a customer could purchase an item that ORDPROC still thinks is in stock.

Write two questions you would ask the business to understand whether this is an accepted risk, a known limitation, or an unrecognized bug. Then write one question you would ask the engineering team to understand whether fixing it is feasible.
