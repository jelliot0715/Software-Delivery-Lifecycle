## Part A — Kanban Planning (~20 minutes)

### Background: Kanban in a Delivery Team

A Kanban board makes work visible. Each card represents one piece of work — usually a user story or task. Cards move left to right through columns that represent stages of your workflow. The key discipline is the **WIP limit**: a cap on how many cards can be in a given column at the same time. WIP limits force the team to finish work before starting new work.

When cards pile up in one column, that column is a **bottleneck**. A bottleneck means work is arriving faster than it can be completed — and everything upstream is waiting. Identifying and resolving bottlenecks is one of the most practical things a BA can do to help a team deliver faster.

---

### Step 1 — Set Up the Board (5 minutes)

Draw or describe a Kanban board with the following columns and WIP limits. (Use paper, a whiteboard, a simple table below, or a tool of your choice.)

| Column | WIP Limit | Purpose |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Backlog** | No limit | All stories not yet started |
| **Ready** | 4 | Stories that have been refined and are ready to be pulled into active work |
| **In Progress** | 3 | Stories actively being developed |
| **In Review / Testing** | 2 | Stories in code review or QA testing |
| **Done** | No limit | Stories deployed or accepted |

> **Why these WIP limits?** The limits above reflect a small team of 3–4 people. In Review is capped at 2 because reviews and testing often block — keeping that column small forces the team to clear reviews before pulling more work.

### Step 2 — Populate the Board (5 minutes)

Using the four user stories you wrote in Lab 5.2 — or the reference stories below if you did not complete Lab 5.2 — place each story in the appropriate column to represent the state of the work at the **start of Sprint 1**.

**Reference Stories (use these if you did not complete Lab 5.2):**

| Story ID | Title | Description |
|----------|-------|-------------|
| ORD-01 | Batch completion notification | As an operations manager, I want an automated alert when ORDPROC finishes, so that I know immediately whether last night's batch was successful or failed. |
| ORD-02 | Structured error logging | As a software engineer, I want ORDPROC to write structured logs in a machine-readable format, so that errors can be filtered, analyzed, and acted on programmatically. |

- **Backlog:** ORD-03, ORD-04
- **Ready:** ORD-01, ORD-02
- **In Progress:** *(empty)*

---

### Step 3 — Simulate the Workflow (7 minutes)

Move the stories through the board following the events below. After each event, update the board and note what you observe.

**Day 1:** The team pulls ORD-01 and ORD-02 into In Progress. (In Progress now has 2 cards — within the WIP limit of 3.)

**Day 3:** ORD-01 is completed and moves to In Review / Testing. ORD-02 is still In Progress.

**Day 4:** ORD-03 is refined and moves from Backlog to Ready. The team wants to start ORD-03 in addition to ORD-02. (In Progress would reach 2 — still within the limit.)

**Day 5:** A reviewer is out sick. ORD-01 is stuck In Review / Testing. The team finishes ORD-02 and moves it to In Review / Testing. (In Review is now at 2 — the WIP limit.)

**Day 6:** ORD-04 is refined and moves to Ready. The team wants to start ORD-03. (In Progress would reach 1 — within the limit. Do they pull it?)

**Day 7:** The reviewer returns. ORD-01 passes review and moves to Done. The team pulls ORD-03 into In Progress.

---

### Step 4 — Answer the Following Questions (3 minutes)

1. On which day did the first bottleneck appear, and in which column?
2. What was the impact of the WIP limit on Day 6? Did it cause a problem, or did it protect the team from a worse problem?
3. At the end of Day 7, what is the state of the board? How many stories are Done?
4. If the team wanted to improve flow, what is one change they could make to the process or the WIP limits?

## Part B — Operational Validation (~20 minutes)

### Background: Closing the Loop

Deploying a solution is not the end of the BA's work — it's the beginning of validation. Once ORDPROC improvements are live, the business will want to know: did we actually fix the problems? Are the requirements being met?

Telemetry — metrics, logs, and traces — is how modern teams answer that question with data instead of intuition. A BA does not need to build dashboards, but they do need to understand what the data says and whether it confirms or contradicts the requirements they wrote.

---
