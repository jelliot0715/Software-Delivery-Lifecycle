# Lab 4.2 – Risk Assessment Workshop

**Duration:** ~40 minutes  
**Format:** Small group exercise

Your instructor will assign your group one of the three scenarios below before you begin.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lab you will be able to:

- Identify and categorize risks across technical, operational, security, compliance, and business dimensions
- Apply a likelihood × impact framework to assess and prioritize risk
- Develop mitigation strategies for high-priority risks
- Communicate risk findings clearly to a non-technical audience
- Compare risk profiles across different types of production changes

---

## Background

Risk assessment is the process of identifying what could go wrong, how likely it is, and how bad it would be. A well-run risk assessment gives a team and its stakeholders a shared, honest picture of exposure before a change goes live — and forces the conversation about whether that exposure is acceptable.

The tool most commonly used to make that picture visual is the risk matrix: a grid that plots likelihood against impact so reviewers can quickly see which risks demand attention and which are acceptable.

In this lab your group will assess a real-world scenario, complete a risk worksheet, plot your findings on a matrix, and present your top risks to the class.

### Scenario A — Production Database Upgrade

The platform engineering team is upgrading the production PostgreSQL database from version 13 to version 15 on a Saturday morning maintenance window (02:00–05:00 UTC). The upgrade affects the primary RDS instance and three read replicas. Estimated downtime is 45–75 minutes.

**Key facts:**
- PostgreSQL 13 reaches end-of-life in November; 4 open CVEs will be resolved by upgrading
- Regression suite passed (847 tests); load testing was conducted at 60% of peak traffic — peak is typically 85–90% on Saturday mornings
- Rollback path is an RDS snapshot restore; estimated RTO is 25–40 minutes
- DBA team provided verbal approval; written sign-off has not been received
- A major marketing campaign launches 10 days after the proposed window

---

### Scenario B — Cloud Migration

A 6-year-old monolithic e-commerce application currently running on on-premises servers is being migrated to AWS. The migration will run in parallel (on-prem and cloud simultaneously) for 30 days before a hard cutover. The application processes customer payments and is in scope for PCI-DSS compliance.

**Key facts:**
- The engineering team has limited AWS production experience; two engineers completed AWS certifications last quarter
- The data center lease expires in 90 days — the cutover date is fixed
- Payment processing will use a new AWS-native payment gateway; the existing gateway will be decommissioned post-cutover
- Data migration involves 4TB of customer and transaction history
- No rollback is possible after cutover due to the data center lease expiration

---

### Scenario C — Black Friday E-commerce Release

A redesigned mobile checkout flow is scheduled to go live 5 days before Black Friday. The new flow includes a redesigned UI, a new mobile payment integration (Apple Pay / Google Pay), and a rewritten order confirmation service.

**Key facts:**
- Regression testing passed on staging; load testing simulated 40% of expected Black Friday peak traffic
- Last year's Black Friday produced a 3× traffic spike versus a normal day
- The previous checkout flow will be removed from the codebase on release day — no feature flag toggle exists
- The payments team that built the new integration has not shipped to production before
- A rollback would require a hotfix deployment and approximately 90 minutes of engineering time

---

## Debrief

After all groups present, discuss as a class:

1. Did different scenarios produce different risk profiles? What drove that — the type of change, the timing, the team involved?
2. Were there risks another group caught that you missed in your scenario?
3. How would you explain your top risk to a business stakeholder who doesn't have a technical background?
4. Scenario B has no rollback path. Does that change how you'd approach the risk assessment? Should no-rollback changes be treated differently by default?
5. At what point would you recommend *not* proceeding with a change? Is there a combination of likelihood and impact that should be an automatic stop?

---

## Summary

In this lab you:

- Analyzed a real-world change scenario through five risk lenses: technical, operational, security, compliance, and business
- Applied likelihood × impact scoring to prioritize risks and surface the ones that matter most
- Plotted a risk matrix and identified critical risks that warrant escalation
- Developed concrete mitigation strategies and assigned ownership
- Compared risk profiles across different change types and heard how other teams approached the same framework

The goal of risk assessment is not to find reasons to block a change — it is to make the decision honest. A team that surfaces its risks clearly and shows credible mitigations earns more trust and faster approvals than one that minimizes or ignores them.
